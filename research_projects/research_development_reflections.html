Reflections on My Learning Throughout the Research Project Process

Engaging in the research project process allowed me to develop a deeper understanding of the stages that shape responsible and rigorous inquiry. As I moved through ethics, methodology, literature reviewing, and data-related tasks, I began to recognise how each component contributes to the credibility, quality, and usefulness of research. My learning was guided not only by course content but also by principles commonly described in research literature, which emphasise ethical responsibility, methodological alignment, and evidence-informed decision-making.

Ethics

My understanding of research ethics developed significantly throughout the project. Ethical frameworks often highlight respect, beneficence, and justice as core principles, and working through the ethics component helped me to see how these principles guide every stage of a study. I became more aware of the responsibilities researchers hold in protecting confidentiality, securing informed consent, and conducting research in ways that minimise risk. Literature on ethical research practices emphasises transparency and accountability, and I found that reflecting on these ideas helped me approach ethics not as a procedural step, but as an ongoing mindset that shapes the integrity of a research study.

Methodology

Learning about methodology deepened my appreciation for how the structure of a study must match its research question. Research theorists often describe methodology as the “logic of the research process”, and this idea became clearer as I examined qualitative, quantitative, and mixed-methods approaches. I learned that the choice of design is more than a technical decision, it determines how meaning is created, how data is interpreted, and how conclusions are drawn. Exploring methodological literature helped me understand that every method carries strengths and limitations, and thoughtful alignment is essential for producing meaningful findings. This shift strengthened my ability to justify methodological choices and understand why certain approaches are more suitable in specific contexts.

Literature Review

The literature review process taught me how evidence forms the foundation of academic inquiry. At first, engaging with large volumes of information felt overwhelming, but as I progressed, I began to understand the importance of identifying patterns, evaluating the credibility of sources, and distinguishing between descriptive information and analytical insight. Research authors often describe literature reviews as a process of mapping an intellectual landscape, and this perspective helped guide my approach. I learned to focus on relevance, quality, and alignment with the research question. This strengthened my synthesis skills and improved my ability to connect ideas in a coherent, academically sound manner.

Data Collection and Engagement with Findings

Although the project did not require full-scale primary data collection, exploring the principles of data collection expanded my understanding of accuracy, reliability, and rigour. Literature commonly highlights the need for clear procedures, consistency in data-handling, and sensitivity to context, particularly in health-related research. Learning about these concepts helped me envision how data is shaped not only by tools and techniques, but also by ethical considerations, participant dynamics, and researcher reflexivity. I also gained insight into how data informs decision-making and how findings must be interpreted within broader systemic and social environments.

Integrating the Learning

One of the key realisations throughout this process was how interconnected these research components are. Ethical considerations influence methodology, methodology shapes data, and literature supports every decision made along the way. Research authors often emphasise that a study is not simply a collection of steps, but a coherent narrative supported by critical thinking, reflection, and alignment. This understanding helped me see research as both a structured and reflective practice.

The research project process expanded my ability to think critically, evaluate evidence, and approach academic work with greater responsibility and methodological awareness. The integration of ethical principles, methodological logic, literature-based grounding, and data-related considerations has strengthened my confidence in navigating research tasks and improved my ability to engage with complex questions in a thoughtful and academically robust way.

Reflection on My Supervision Process

My supervision process played a meaningful role in shaping my learning throughout this course. Working with both Dr Heleen Roos and Storm Makings provided me with a balanced supervisory environment that combined guidance, structure, and opportunities for independent growth. Their different supervisory strengths complemented each other and helped me develop confidence in engaging with research concepts, refining my academic writing, and approaching the research process with clarity and focus.

From the beginning of the course, Dr Roos offered a sense of academic grounding. Her guidance often centred on helping me understand the conceptual foundations that support strong research, including the importance of aligning research questions, methodology, and ethical considerations. Her feedback encouraged me to think more critically about how decisions in a research project influence its coherence and overall credibility. This supported my ability to approach each task with a stronger sense of purpose and academic reasoning.

Working with Storm Makings added an important layer to my supervision experience. Her approach was characterised by practical support, attentiveness, and a willingness to clarify uncertainties in a way that made complex concepts more approachable. She helped me understand how to break research tasks into manageable components and supported me in developing the confidence needed to navigate unfamiliar academic expectations. Her guidance was especially helpful when balancing full-time work with postgraduate responsibilities, as she created space for questions and reflection without judgement.

A significant part of my development came through completing the GOLD01 postgraduate certificate course, which focused on navigating the supervisor student relationship. This course helped me understand that supervision is not only about receiving guidance, but also about developing the skills to communicate effectively, set realistic expectations, manage feedback, and take ownership of my learning journey. The literature and tools introduced in GOLD01 highlighted that successful supervision relies on mutual respect, consistent engagement, and clarity of roles. Applying these insights helped me become more intentional in preparing for meetings, articulating my challenges, and reflecting on feedback in a constructive way.

The combination of supportive supervision and the structured learning provided through GOLD01 shaped my approach to this course in several important ways. It strengthened my ability to work independently while remaining open to guidance. It taught me the value of reflective communication and helped me recognise the supervisor–student relationship as an essential part of academic development rather than merely an administrative requirement. I also gained a clearer understanding of how supervision contributes to research integrity, researcher identity, and academic confidence.

The supervision process contributed significantly to my growth as an emerging researcher. It encouraged me to think more deeply, engage more consistently, and approach research with a greater sense of responsibility and awareness. The experience reinforced the idea that supervision is a collaborative and reflective journey one that supports not only academic success but also the development of long-term research competencies.

Reflection on My Evolving Research Identity

My research identity has developed significantly throughout this course, shaped by continuous engagement with research concepts, supervisory guidance, reflective practice, and the balance of academic responsibilities with full-time professional work. At the beginning of the year, I saw myself primarily as a practitioner who was learning about research from the outside. Over time, my understanding of what it means to be a researcher has shifted, and I now recognise myself as someone who can engage with research more confidently, purposefully, and with greater awareness of the responsibilities it carries.

Engaging with methodology, ethics, literature reviewing, and project planning helped me understand that being a researcher is not just about mastering technical skills. It requires curiosity, critical thinking, and a willingness to explore questions with openness. As I progressed through the course, I noticed that my approach to problems became more analytical. I began asking clearer questions, being more intentional in selecting sources, and becoming more reflective about the reasoning behind my decisions. This shift marked the beginnings of a more defined research identity.

Developing this identity was strengthened by the feedback and guidance I received from my supervisors, Dr Heleen Roos and Storm Makings. Their support encouraged me to think more deeply about the purpose of my work and the role of the researcher in producing ethically sound, well-reasoned, and meaningful contributions. Their encouragement to reflect, revise, and refine my thinking helped me see research as a collaborative and iterative process rather than a set of isolated tasks. Learning to engage with feedback in a constructive way also formed an important part of how I now understand myself as a researcher.

My evolving identity was also shaped by the GOLD01 postgraduate supervision course, which expanded my understanding of the researcher’s role within a broader academic community. The course highlighted the importance of self-regulation, accountability, autonomy, and professional maturity in research. These principles helped me recognise that a research identity is built through intentional actions preparation, reflection, responsible scholarship, and the ability to communicate research-related decisions clearly.

Balancing work with my Honours studies contributed to this development in a unique way. It required discipline, planning, and a strong sense of ownership over my learning. Through this, I began to see myself not only as someone completing academic requirements, but as someone capable of contributing to structured knowledge-making processes. This sense of capability has grown gradually as I engaged more deeply with research literature and began to understand how my thinking fits into larger conversations within the health sciences.

Today, I see myself as an emerging researcher who values ethical conduct, methodological clarity, and thoughtful engagement with evidence. I recognise that research is a continuous learning journey and that developing a research identity is an evolving process shaped by experience, reflection, and practice. I feel more confident in approaching research questions, more aware of the systems that shape knowledge production, and more prepared to contribute meaningfully in both academic and professional environments.

This evolving identity will continue to influence how I approach future projects, particularly as I take on increasing responsibilities in my professional role. I now see research as an integral part of my growth one that strengthens my ability to think critically, communicate effectively, and engage with complex issues in a systematic and reflective way.
