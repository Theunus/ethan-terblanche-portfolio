Artefact 1 – Innovation Image Reflection

Competency Link

Communicator

Context

This artefact required me to search for an image online that reflected how I feel about innovation, and to explain why it represented my perspective. The task was designed as an introductory reflection on the concept of innovation in health systems, encouraging me to translate abstract feelings and ideas into visual representation and language.

Rationale

I selected this artefact because it captures my initial engagement with the concept of innovation in a creative and expressive format. It highlights my ability to communicate complex emotions and ideas through a combination of visual and written mediums. By linking an image to innovation, I began to appreciate how symbolic representation can serve as an accessible means of communication, which is crucial in health contexts where diverse audiences must be engaged.

Reflection

The most challenging aspect of this task was identifying a single image that could encompass the multifaceted nature of innovation. Innovation is dynamic, disruptive, and often uncertain, so choosing one visual meant narrowing down to a specific theme that resonated personally. The most rewarding element was realising that effective communication is not only about factual accuracy but also about emotional resonance. This artefact demonstrates my growth in the competency of communication by showing how I can adapt abstract ideas into accessible forms. In future coursework and professional practice, I will apply this learning by seeking creative methods to engage stakeholders, for example using visuals, infographics, or metaphors when discussing complex health system challenges.

Next Steps

I aim to develop further skills in visual communication and health literacy, ensuring that I can translate technical and theoretical ideas into accessible formats for both academic and public audiences.

Artefact 2 – Defining Health Innovation

Competency Link

Systems Thinking

Context

This artefact focused on defining health innovation by considering sustaining and disruptive innovations, and exploring the multiple lenses through which the health system can be studied. It also required the creation of a visual representation to demonstrate the transformative impact of a disruptive innovation in healthcare. This activity was completed collaboratively in groups, where we discussed definitions and adapted them to reflect shared understanding.

Rationale

I selected this artefact because it represents my early engagement with systems thinking. By revisiting and refining a definition of health innovation, I was challenged to think critically about the layers and dynamics of the health system. The process of differentiating between sustaining and disruptive innovations encouraged me to see health systems as adaptive and multifaceted rather than static. This artefact evidences my competency in systems thinking by requiring me to connect different perspectives (technological, social, economic) into a coherent understanding. The visual representation reinforced the ability to synthesise complex concepts into accessible formats.

Reflection

The most challenging aspect of this task was articulating a definition of health innovation that balanced clarity with comprehensiveness. Health innovation spans technology, culture, systems, and policy; reducing it to a single definition risked oversimplification. However, through group collaboration, we were able to integrate multiple viewpoints, which broadened my appreciation for the systemic nature of innovation. The most rewarding part was realising that disruptive innovation can catalyse large-scale system transformation, especially when it addresses accessibility and cost in resource-limited contexts. This artefact shows my growth in systems thinking, as I learned to recognise that innovations are embedded within larger structures and influence multiple layers of healthcare delivery. I will apply this learning in professional contexts by ensuring that when evaluating an innovation, I do not only consider the immediate benefits but also the systemic ripple effects.

Next Steps

My next step is to deepen my understanding of how health innovations diffuse within systems and across contexts, particularly in low- and middle-income countries. I aim to explore models such as Diffusion of Innovation theory to strengthen my systemic perspective.

Artefact 3 – AI Advancements in Healthcare

Competency Link

Scholarship

Context

This artefact required me to reflect on artificial intelligence (AI) advancements that have already impacted healthcare. I was asked to consider how these technologies contribute to improving patient outcomes, drawing on examples discussed in class and wider literature.

Rationale

I chose this artefact because it evidences my scholarly engagement with contemporary issues in health systems. It demonstrates my ability to identify and critically examine innovations that are shaping patient care. Examples such as AI-driven diagnostic tools and predictive analytics illustrate how technology is applied to improve outcomes, yet they also raise questions around evidence, ethics, and equity. This artefact links to the competency of scholarship as it required me to engage with literature, case studies, and class discussions, synthesising them into informed reflection.

Reflection

The most challenging part of this artefact was moving beyond a descriptive account of AI advancements towards a critical evaluation of their impact. Initially, I was impressed by the promise of AI to improve efficiency, but I soon recognised that the evidence base is nuanced, with studies showing variable effectiveness across contexts. The most rewarding aspect was learning how evidence-based scholarship can guide responsible adoption. This artefact represents growth in my scholarly competency, as I was required to interrogate both the potential and limitations of technology. I will apply this learning by continuing to engage critically with literature, ensuring that I do not take innovations at face value, but instead examine their empirical support and contextual applicability.

Next Steps

In relation to scholarship, my next step is to strengthen my ability to appraise emerging technologies by developing skills in critical appraisal, using frameworks such as PRISMA or GRADE when reviewing evidence.

Artefact 4 – Solutions to Contemporary Health Challenges

Competency Link

Responsive Decision-Making

Context

This artefact involved preparing a discussion around possible solutions to contemporary health challenges, with a particular focus on scalability, environmental impact, and energy efficiency. The activity required me to consider how these challenges intersect with innovation and to propose approaches that balance feasibility, sustainability, and broader system implications.

Rationale

I selected this artefact because it captures my ability to make responsive and informed decisions within the context of health systems. The exercise required weighing multiple, often competing, factors such as sustainability, scalability, and environmental responsibility. In healthcare, decision-making is rarely straightforward; this task demonstrated how complexity must be addressed in a structured and thoughtful manner. By linking my proposed solutions to issues such as energy efficiency, I demonstrated awareness of the wider determinants of health and the systemic implications of health innovations.

Reflection

The most challenging aspect of this task was considering the trade-offs inherent in real-world solutions. For example, an intervention that is highly scalable may have significant environmental costs, while one that is environmentally friendly may be difficult to scale in low-resource settings. The most rewarding aspect was realising that responsive decision-making involves not only choosing a course of action but also justifying it in relation to ethical, social, and systemic considerations. This artefact evidences my growth by showing that I can approach problems holistically, anticipate unintended consequences, and adapt decisions to context. Going forward, I will apply this competency by integrating sustainability assessments into my evaluation of health innovations, ensuring that solutions are not only effective but also responsible.

Next Steps

My next step is to develop expertise in decision-making frameworks such as health technology assessment (HTA), which will allow me to systematically evaluate innovations across dimensions of effectiveness, cost, equity, and sustainability.

Artefact 5 – Ethical Principles in Innovation

Competency Link

Change Agent

Context

This artefact asked me to reflect on how ethical principles and challenges have influenced the way I think about innovation in health systems. It built upon prior class discussions and readings on bioethics, encouraging me to critically examine the moral dimensions of health innovation.

Rationale

I selected this artefact because it demonstrates my growing competency as a change agent. Understanding ethical principles is central to advocating for and implementing responsible innovations. By critically reflecting on principles such as justice, beneficence, autonomy, and non-maleficence, I was able to evaluate how ethical considerations should guide decision-making in innovation. This artefact highlights that change agents are not only implementers of innovation but also custodians of ethical practice, ensuring that innovations do not exacerbate inequalities or harm vulnerable populations.

Reflection

The most challenging part of this task was confronting the tension between innovation and ethics. On one hand, innovations promise efficiency and improved outcomes; on the other, they may introduce risks or unintended harms. This required me to critically evaluate my own assumptions about innovation as an inherently positive force. The most rewarding aspect was realising that ethics can be a driver of innovation: constraints such as equity or justice can inspire more creative and socially responsive solutions. This artefact demonstrates my growth by showing how I moved from viewing ethics as a constraint to recognising it as an enabler of responsible change. I will apply this learning in professional contexts by advocating for ethical reviews and stakeholder engagement in any innovation project.

Next Steps

I intend to strengthen my understanding of applied bioethics and health equity frameworks so that I can act more effectively as a change agent, ensuring that future innovations are both impactful and ethically grounded.

Artefact 6 – Concept Paper on a Contemporary Health Challenge

Competency Link

Systems Thinking & Change Agent

Context

This artefact built upon an earlier assignment where I identified a contemporary health challenge. For this task, I worked collaboratively to develop an innovative concept addressing Type 2 Diabetes as a pressing issue in South Africa. The group produced a comprehensive report proposing integrated solutions spanning education, community engagement, technology, policy reform, and business innovation. The concept paper reflected a multidimensional understanding of the issue, recognising the complexity of diabetes as a non-communicable disease with behavioural, socio-economic, and systemic determinants.

Rationale

I selected this artefact because it exemplifies systems thinking and demonstrates my role as a change agent in addressing health challenges. The report went beyond technical fixes to integrate social, cultural, and organisational innovations. For example, proposed interventions included compulsory school gardens, the creation of a mobile application (The Vital App), community-driven Diabetes Day SA events, and the development of healthier business models such as VitaBites Cafés. By combining these strands, we aimed to achieve long-term behavioural change and improve public health outcomes. This artefact evidences the competency of systems thinking as it required consideration of multiple determinants and systemic interactions. It also links to the change agent competency, as it involved envisioning practical, scalable, and ethically responsible interventions that could transform health behaviours and environments.

Reflection

The most challenging aspect of this assignment was synthesising diverse forms of innovation, technological, cultural, organisational, and social, into a cohesive model. It required balancing feasibility with creativity, as well as grounding proposals in evidence while maintaining innovative ambition. The collaborative nature of the task was rewarding, as each group member brought unique insights, leading to richer, multidimensional solutions. This artefact demonstrates my growth in both systems thinking and leadership, as I learned how to engage with stakeholders conceptually and design inclusive, sustainable interventions. I was particularly struck by the importance of cultural sensitivity and stakeholder participation, which we embedded through mechanisms such as Community Health Design Labs. In professional contexts, I will apply this learning by ensuring that any innovation is approached holistically, aligning with both technical and social realities, and by positioning myself as an advocate for equity-driven change.

Next Steps

Building on this artefact, I plan to strengthen my skills in implementation science and programme evaluation. I aim to learn how to move innovative concepts from design to practice, ensuring they achieve measurable outcomes and long-term sustainability.

Artefact 7 – WHO Report on Ethics and Governance of AI in Health

Competency Link

Scholarship & Responsive Decision-Making

Context

This artefact required me to critically engage with the World Health Organization’s (2021) report on the Ethics and Governance of Artificial Intelligence for Health. I was tasked with responding to three specific questions: (1) identifying the most difficult ethical principle to implement in practice, (2) reflecting on the greatest risks posed by AI adoption in South Africa, and (3) exploring what meaningful participation in AI design might look like in the South African context. The assignment demanded approximately 150–200-word responses for each question, evidencing my ability to apply global frameworks to local realities.

Rationale

I selected this artefact because it illustrates the development of my scholarly and decision-making competencies. By engaging directly with an authoritative policy document, I demonstrated the ability to interpret, critique, and contextualise international guidance within a South African healthcare system. This artefact was particularly significant because it required me to grapple with ethical challenges such as transparency, accountability, inclusiveness, and justice. Linking these principles to practical realities, such as digital literacy barriers or inequities in data, allowed me to demonstrate an applied understanding of how ethics must inform decisions. This aligns strongly with the responsive decision-making competency, as it demanded careful reasoning in conditions of uncertainty and complexity.

Reflection

The most challenging aspect of this task was evaluating the principle of transparency and explainability. AI algorithms often operate as ‘black boxes’, making it difficult for patients and providers to understand how decisions are generated. This challenge is amplified in South Africa, where limited technical capacity and resource constraints complicate oversight. The most rewarding part of the assignment was identifying strategies for inclusiveness and participation. I was able to explore how diverse stakeholders, including patients, community leaders, policymakers, and healthcare workers, could be engaged in co-design processes. This artefact evidences my growth by showing that I can integrate ethical principles into practical decision-making frameworks, balancing ideals with feasibility. In future coursework and professional practice, I will apply this by promoting participatory approaches, especially when evaluating or implementing new technologies. I also realised that risks such as bias and inequality are not abstract but have concrete impacts on vulnerable groups, and thus must be central to decision-making.

Next Steps

My next step is to expand my knowledge of digital ethics and governance frameworks. I aim to develop the ability to critically appraise AI systems not only from a technical perspective but also from a human rights and equity standpoint.

I selected this artefact because it demonstrates my competency as both a communicator and a change agent. It also reflects growth as a change agent, as I moved from identifying problems to advocating for practical, ethical safeguards. Integrating scholarly literature (e.g., Obermeyer et al., 2019; Rajkomar et al., 2018; London, 2019) with professional and social contexts demonstrated that my reflections are grounded in evidence while oriented towards real-world application.
