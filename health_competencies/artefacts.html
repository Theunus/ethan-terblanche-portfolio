Interview: Junior Hospital Management Officer (Job Advertisement)

1. Context

The artefact I have selected is my participation in an interview for the Junior Hospital Management Officer (Health Systems Strengthening) position at Charlotte Maxeke Academic Hospital. The interview was conducted by a panel of senior staff members, including individuals involved in hospital management, clinical governance, and health systems strengthening. I chose this artefact because it represents an important moment in my transition from academic learning to the practical environment of the South African public health system. It allowed me to engage directly with the real-world expectations of a health system strengthening role, particularly one aligned with the competencies I have developed throughout my Health Systems Science Honours programme. The job advert highlighted several elements that resonated with my coursework, including a focus on improving service delivery, patient experience, operational coordination, and quality of care. It explicitly targeted recent graduates in Health Systems Science and emphasised competencies such as systems thinking, analytical skills, collaborative work, ethical conduct, and a willingness to develop leadership capacity . This alignment made the interview meaningful because it required me to demonstrate these competencies in a professional and applied context.

2. Reflection

The interview contributed significantly to my development and understanding of Health Systems Science competencies. Preparing for the interview required me to revisit core concepts such as hospital performance indicators, patient flow, clinical governance, complaint management, and quality improvement, all of which were highlighted in the role description . This strengthened my ability to connect theoretical learning with real operational challenges. One of the key reflections from this artefact relates to systems thinking. During the interview, the panel asked questions about improving continuity of care, reducing waiting times, and supporting patient safety audits. These questions required me to recognise how different components of the system are interconnected. This reflects the World Health Organization’s view that health systems operate through interacting building blocks and that changes in one area influence others (WHO 2007). My responses drew on what I had learned in the honours programme about complexity, interdependence and the need to think beyond isolated units. The interview also prompted reflection on my communication and collaboration skills. Engaging with a panel required clarity, confidence and an appreciation for multidisciplinary perspectives. This linked directly to literature showing that effective teamwork is essential for safe, high-quality health care (Reeves et al. 2017). I realised that the honours programme had prepared me to interact professionally and present ideas based on evidence and structured reasoning. Another competency strengthened by this artefact was ethical and professional conduct. The role stresses respect, professionalism and ethical reasoning as core expectations . Reflecting on the interview, I recognised how my training in ethical frameworks, fairness and patient-centred decision making shaped the way I approached questions about patient complaints, service responsiveness and community engagement. Literature by Beauchamp and Childress (2019) emphasises that ethical practice requires awareness of autonomy, justice and beneficence. These principles influenced how I framed my responses. The artefact also reinforced my leadership awareness. Even though the role targets recent graduates, the career pathway described in the advert highlights development toward future leadership roles such as Clinical Governance Coordinator and Hospital CEO . Reflecting on this encouraged me to think about how my confidence and decision-making abilities have grown and how I might continue developing these competencies. The interview helped me understand the importance of analytical and evidence-informed decision making. Questions about problem solving and performance indicators required me to think critically and apply data-driven reasoning. This aligns with Greenhalgh’s (2014) perspective that analytical skills strengthen decision making in health systems. This artefact was valuable because it placed me in a setting where I had to apply the competencies I have been developing throughout the programme. It reinforced my readiness to transition into the health system and highlighted areas for continued growth.

Personal Statement: My Developing Professional Identity

My understanding of my professional identity has grown significantly throughout my Health Systems Science training. The process of developing my personal statement has shown me how my values, skills and attitudes have evolved across three key areas. These areas are empathy, critical thinking and ethical action. Each of these domains has influenced the way I see myself and the way I hope to contribute to the health system.

Empathy

My initial view of empathy was rooted in the idea of being present with another person’s experience and listening without the need to fix or interpret their reality. I associated empathy with presence, dignity and connection. I valued the ability to listen actively and allow space for others, which I believed helped prevent misunderstanding and supported meaningful relationships. This understanding is reflected in my original statement, where I emphasised curiosity and patience as foundations for connection . After working through the learning content, I realised that empathy is not something one either has or does not have. It is a skill that requires regular practice. Discussions and readings, including perspectives such as Brené Brown’s distinction between empathy and sympathy, helped me recognise that empathy involves boundaries and mindful engagement. Indigenous worldviews further expanded my understanding by emphasising accompaniment rather than absorbing another person’s pain. This showed me that empathy is relational and contextual rather than purely emotional. Reflecting on this, I now see empathy as a practice that requires presence, self-awareness and the ability to remain comfortable in silence or discomfort. My refined understanding highlights that empathy is not about seeing inside someone’s emotions but about being willing to stay with them in their experience with dignity and respect .

Critical Thinking

Critical thinking initially meant pausing, questioning and seeking deeper meaning in what I encountered. I saw it as a process of engaging with uncertainty while drawing on knowledge of power, systems and language. I valued truth seeking and accountability and relied on skills such as identifying assumptions, comparing perspectives and noticing contradictions. My early approach focused on responsible use of knowledge and avoiding unexamined repetition of ideas . Through readings and discussions that explored decoloniality and knowledge production, my understanding shifted. I learned that critical thinking is not limited to pointing out errors or identifying logical weaknesses. It is about understanding the origins of knowledge and interrogating whose voices are heard and whose are excluded. Thinkers such as Frantz Fanon and Walter Mignolo influenced my appreciation of how social structures shape knowledge. This moved my focus from judging individuals to examining broader systems. As a result, I now see critical thinking as a way of being more than a method of analysis. It requires openness, humility and the willingness to sit with discomfort. Silence and listening also form part of this practice. I have come to recognise that critical thinking is both reflective and relational .

Ethical Action

My early philosophy of ethical action centred on doing what is right, even when unnoticed or difficult. I associated ethical behaviour with principles that protect the good of both myself and others. I believed ethical action required knowledge of moral theories and the ability to identify tensions and potential consequences. I valued honesty, responsibility and courage. This understanding guided how I approached decision making and conflict . Through engagement with the course content, my understanding deepened. I had originally viewed ethics as a set of rules that offered clear answers. Through exploring relational and community based ethical theories, I learned that ethical action often arises in situations where the right answer is not clear. Ethical decisions rely on reflection, context and presence. I also became aware of the importance of relationships and responsiveness. The ethics of care literature emphasised that ethical behaviour involves attention to connection, responsibility and the needs of others. These perspectives helped me recognise that ethical action is an evolving practice shaped by who I am accountable to and the environments in which I work. Ethical living is not about perfection but about returning to reflection and learning repeatedly

Integrated Reflection

Across empathy, critical thinking and ethical action, I can see that my professional identity has shifted from relying on personal instinct to embracing reflective and intentional practice. Each competency has shaped the way I think, act and relate to others. I now understand my role in health systems as one requiring continuous learning, openness and humility. These competencies support meaningful engagement with patients, colleagues and communities and prepare me to contribute responsibly to the broader goals of equity and system strengthening.

The artefact I selected is my Johari’s Window self-assessment exercise. This artefact includes four components that map my self-perception and how others perceive me. The sections displayed in my document include the Open Area, Blind Spot, Hidden Area and Unknown Area. Each quadrant captures insights about my dependability, empathy, creative strengths, leadership presence, resilience, personal vulnerabilities and unrealised potential . I chose this artefact because it offered a structured method for exploring my self-awareness and identity. It encouraged me to reflect not only on how I view myself, but also on how peers, colleagues and those close to me experience my behaviour, communication and emerging competencies. The inclusion of feedback from others, captured through a Google Form, further deepened the assessment. Responses highlighted competencies such as communication, collaboration, adaptability, health advocacy, ethical competence and leadership, which are central to Health Systems Science. These responses were summarised in the table on page 3, showing both strengths and areas for improvement in aspects such as composure, transparency and openness to alternative approaches . This artefact aligns well with the objectives of the Health Systems Science curriculum as it supports reflective practice, emotional intelligence, communication awareness and leadership development.

2. Reflection

Engaging with this Johari Window activity helped me understand myself more clearly, both in personal and professional contexts. The Open Area affirmed qualities that I already recognised, such as dependability, empathy and creativity. These traits appear in the first quadrant of my artefact, where I describe myself as caring, reflective and committed to others. It was meaningful to see these strengths mirrored by people who know me well, which is consistent with the statements in the Open Area on page 2 . Reflecting on the Blind Spot allowed me to consider the qualities that others see but that I may overlook. For example, the document notes that people perceive me as a natural leader, someone who brings individuals together and offers stability in uncertain situations. The feedback on page 3 supports this, where respondents strongly agreed that I collaborate well, maintain composure under pressure and encourage independent thinking . Recognising these perceptions helped me understand that leadership is not only about formal roles. It also involves everyday actions such as listening, organising and creating safe spaces for others. This insight contributed to my development of the leadership awareness competency in Health Systems Science. The Hidden Area encouraged deeper introspection. I noted private emotions, insecurities and the emotional toll of caregiving. This aligns with the reflective passage on page 4, where I acknowledge that some of my anxieties remain unshared. Reflecting on this helped me understand the importance of boundary setting and emotional awareness in health systems work. Professionals often carry emotional labour quietly, and recognising this in myself supports healthier patterns of resilience. The Unknown Area allowed me to consider possibilities and undiscovered aspects of myself. On page 2, I describe unrealised potential and unexplored interests. This quadrant helped me appreciate that personal growth is ongoing and that new strengths may emerge as my career and life experience evolve. What strengthened this artefact further was the structured feedback from others. The table on page 3 highlighted competencies such as communication, ethical and cultural competence, adaptability, creativity and health advocacy as essential for becoming a stronger health systems thinker. These responses helped me understand how people experience my contributions in group settings and how these competencies align with the broader literature on effective practice in health systems. This process taught me that self-awareness is foundational for work in complex systems. It enhances interpersonal relationships, supports ethical decision making and strengthens professional identity. The reflective process captured on page 5 describes this journey as one of tenderness and curiosity. I now see myself as a work in progress, open to learning and capable of continuous growth in both personal and professional spheres

Completion of Eight Postgraduate GOLD01 Certificates

Context

This artefact represents the completion of eight postgraduate GOLD01 research-based certificates. These short learning programmes focused on research skills, academic development and system-level understanding. I chose this artefact because completing eight separate certificates demonstrates consistency, discipline and a commitment to strengthening my academic foundation across multiple research domains. It also reflects my interest in expanding my capacity to engage with evidence, interpret data and contribute meaningfully to the broader field of Health Systems Science.

Reflection

Completing eight research-based certificates allowed me to build confidence in my academic abilities and strengthened several Health Systems Science competencies. Each certificate required independent learning, engagement with academic literature and the application of research principles. This aligned with my development in evidence-informed decision making, critical interpretation and analytical reasoning. The repeated practice helped me refine my understanding of research structures, improve my writing skills and become more comfortable engaging with unfamiliar material.

The process also strengthened competencies such as adaptability and reflective practice. Moving through eight certificates required time management, self-motivation and the ability to adjust to different formats and expectations. I became more aware of my learning preferences and my ability to work through challenges without external prompting. Completing the certificates also reinforced the importance of continuous learning within health systems. It reminded me that research is a key driver of quality improvement, equity and system strengthening.

These certificates contributed to my growth by expanding my research capabilities and building the confidence needed to apply these skills in real-world settings. I now have a stronger foundation for academic work, postgraduate study or roles that require technical research skills.
