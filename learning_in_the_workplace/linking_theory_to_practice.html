Linking Theory to Practice

During my internship in the Marketing and Student Recruitment division, I often found myself drawing on theories and concepts from my Honours coursework. Applying academic learning in a real organisational environment allowed me to see how theory translates into action and how it guides decision making, problem solving, and interpersonal interaction. This section explains how theoretical concepts from the Honours modules connected to the work I carried out at NWU and how these theories shaped my understanding of the workplace.

1. Organisational Culture Theory in Practice

In my Organisational Culture assignment, I explored how values, behaviours, and structures influence the flow of work within a department. The theories of Schein, Cameron and Quinn, and Deal and Kennedy provided frameworks for understanding why teams behave as they do and how culture can either enable or obstruct collaboration. When I examined the workplace with these lenses, I began to understand why our department functioned well: there was a clear blend of task culture and role culture, supported by shared values and regular communication. The structured approval processes reflected formal role culture, while creative brainstorming and campaign planning reflected task culture where teamwork and problem solving were encouraged. Recognising these patterns helped me navigate the environment more effectively because I understood how decisions were made and why certain processes existed. My assignment analysis allowed me to match what I experienced each day with established organisational theory .

2. Leadership Theory Observed in Daily Work

Leadership theory became particularly relevant when I began observing the leadership style in my department. Through completing the Leadership Styles assignment and reflecting on transformational and democratic leadership, I could clearly see how my line manager embodied these approaches. He encouraged participation, listened to input from the team, and created opportunities for me to grow through autonomy and constructive feedback. Applying leadership theory helped me make sense of why the team was motivated and why communication flowed effectively. In practice, I experienced the principles of psychological safety described by Edmondson, because I felt confident to ask questions and share ideas without fear of criticism. The theory also guided my reflection on the leader I hope to become in my future career, highlighting empathy, clear communication, and vision as essential attributes .

3. Conflict Resolution Concepts in Real Workplace Dynamics

In the Conflict Resolution assignment, I applied frameworks such as the Thomas Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument and Rahim's Integrative Conflict Management Model to analyse a disagreement between teams in my workplace. These frameworks made it easier to identify the root causes of conflict, which were often linked to unclear communication or mismatched expectations. When conflict resurfaced, I could identify whether the response leaned towards avoidance, compromise, or attempted collaboration. Understanding these patterns helped me develop more neutral language and active listening skills during moments of tension. I learned that conflict resolution is not only about solving the immediate issue but also about maintaining relationships and preventing similar problems from recurring. This encouraged me to communicate more clearly and check understanding when coordinating design briefs and deadlines, which improved workflow across the team .

4. Systems Thinking in Understanding NWU as an Organisation

Systems thinking was central to understanding how NWU operates as a large multi campus institution. Through analysing PEST and VMOST frameworks in my internship report, I realised how internal tasks linked to external pressures such as funding, policy regulation, digital transformation, and student expectations. Viewing the university as a system helped me understand why marketing campaigns had strategic timing, why brand consistency mattered, and why our communication had to align with the broader mission. It also taught me that no project operates in isolation. For example, recruitment campaigns had strategic importance because they influenced student enrolment targets, financial stability, and institutional visibility. This perspective helped me respond more effectively to deadlines and recognise the significance of quality control in design outputs .

5. Communication Theory Applied to Project Coordination

Communication theory from the Honours programme emphasised clarity, audience awareness, and the importance of structured messaging. These concepts were helpful every time I translated a client's needs into a brief for designers. I learned to simplify complex instructions, write concise emails, and confirm understanding when necessary. This prevented miscommunication and reduced revision cycles for design tasks. It also helped me establish credibility with colleagues because accurate and respectful communication is central to effective project coordination. Observing how communication influenced workflow demonstrated that theoretical concepts from health systems communication are directly applicable in professional environments.

6. Ethical and Equity Considerations in Workplace Behaviour

Modules that focused on equity, diversity, and ethical practice shaped how I approached interactions within the department. My diversity assignment encouraged me to recognise strengths and gaps in inclusion efforts, which guided how I communicated with colleagues from different backgrounds and how I viewed organisational responsibility. I became more aware of the need to create space for all voices in meetings and to acknowledge different working styles and cultural perspectives. This helped me adopt a more empathetic and patient approach in my work, strengthening professional relationships and contributing to a more inclusive environment .

7. Applying Project Management Concepts in a Real Environment

Many of the practical skills I used daily such as prioritising tasks, coordinating timelines, and managing deliverables were informed by project management principles taught during the programme. The internship became a practical classroom where I tested these theories. For example, understanding task sequencing helped me break down large campaigns into manageable parts. Knowledge of risk identification helped me foresee potential delays and communicate early with stakeholders. These theories supported my ability to deliver work consistently and meet the expectations set out in my internship report KPIs.

Conclusion

Linking theory to practice allowed me to experience the value of academic learning in real time. Theories that might appear abstract in the classroom came to life through practical application at NWU. This integration strengthened my understanding of organisational dynamics, leadership, communication, and health systems principles. More importantly, it shaped my awareness of the type of professional I want to become and the values I want to carry into future roles.
